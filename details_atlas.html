Executive Summary

Atlas is an innovative trip planning application designed to streamline the travel planning process. It customizes itineraries based on user inputs such as destination, budget, and dates. The app's development focused on creating an efficient and versatile search method that caters to diverse travel preferences. Atlas not only allows users to plan their entire trip but also individual aspects, accommodating various travel styles, budget considerations, destination choices, and desired activities. The primary goals are to:

  • Enhance the user experience with a powerful search tool.
  • Offer adaptable planning options for all types of travelers.

Method

The development of Atlas fully embraced the Goal-Directed Design (GDD) method, a systematic approach crafted by Alan Cooper. GDD was the cornerstone of our design process from inception to completion. Typically, this process starts with stakeholder and kickoff meetings and culminates in the final product. Given the 16-week timeframe of our Spring semester, we adapted GDD to fit our project's timeline. We diligently followed these phases:

  • Reseach: Understanding users and the domain.
  • Modeling: Creating user personas and context of use.
  • Requirements: Identifying and defining the needs of users, business, and technology.
  • FrameworkFramework: Establishing the design's structure and workflow.
  • Refinement: Enhancing behaviors, aesthetics, and content.

The primary objective of this design approach was to ensure that the app aligns with user goals at every stage. The subsequent sections offer an in-depth exploration of each phase, emphasizing how they collectively contribute to creating a user-centric and goal-oriented design.

Research

Kickoff Meeting:
In our simulated Kickoff Meeting for the Atlas app, we initially targeted budget-conscious college students for our travel app, later expanding to a broader audience including business travelers and leisure seekers. This phase involved formulating a problem statement and assumptions, with a focus on developing unique features for budget-friendly and efficient travel planning, while ensuring the app's interface caters to the diverse needs of our expanded user base.

Literature Review:
Our Literature Review delved into the psychology of travel and generational preferences, aligning travel motivations with Maslow's hierarchy of needs and highlighting differences between Gen Z's eco-conscious, experience-driven travel and Millennials' resource-enabled frequent travels. This research guides our app's development to address varied traveler needs, considering factors like age, travel objectives, and time constraints, aiming for a universally appealing and functional travel experience.

Competitive Audit:
The Competitive Audit analyzed major travel apps like Airbnb and Kayak, identifying gaps in user experience enhancements. We also explored niche apps like AllTrails and Naver, understanding the importance of a multi-start search route and detailed filters in our app. The findings from this audit, along with simulated stakeholder inputs, inform our design strategy to integrate comprehensive booking options and target a diverse user base, measuring success through user engagement and profitability.

User Research:
In the User Research phase, our team broadened the target audience using persona hypothesis templates and engaged in interviews with travel enthusiasts, aiming to tailor the Atlas app to genuine user requirements. This phase emphasized the crucial distinction between the objectives of designers and the needs of users, guiding us to create a product that genuinely resonates with users by comprehending their thought processes and interactions with travel products. The insights gathered from these interviews were instrumental in forming a user-focused and pragmatic design approach. Moreover, this phase highlighted the value of empathetic design, as understanding and incorporating user feedback was key to refining our app's functionality and usability.

Modeling

In the Modeling Phase, our team analyzed interview data to identify patterns and define user goals, leading to the creation of user personas. These personas, Marvin and Madeline, were crafted based on common behaviors and preferences found in our research. Marvin, our Primary Persona, is driven by the desire for unique experiences and cultural learning, aiming to visit numerous places. Madeline, our Secondary Persona, seeks a balance between work and leisure travel, focusing on business efficiency and accumulating travel rewards for personal trips. Our design primarily addresses Marvin's needs while also accommodating Madeline's, ensuring both personas' goals are met without compromise.

  • In the user research phase, the team analyzed interview data from five users, identifying two distinct clusters of travel preferences. The first cluster (1&3), labeled as pink, consisted of leisure travelers who often travel, seek unique experiences, plan trips quickly, and prioritize uniqueness in their travels. This group's flexible and spontaneous travel style led to their characteristics being chosen for our Primary Persona.   • Defining Persona Goals
    Upon identifying behavior patterns and Persona goals, the team progressed to constructing the Personas. Utilizing a random name generator, we distilled the identified clusters into specific bullet points, clearly defining the end goals for both the Primary and Secondary Personas. This step was crucial as it provided clear direction for the app's development, aligning its features and functionalities with the specific needs and aspirations of these Personas. This process of defining Persona goals effectively laid the groundwork for the subsequent Requirements Phase, ensuring a targeted and user-centric approach in our app design.

**ADD PICS** Requirements

In the Modeling Phase, the team broke down the Requirements Phase into key steps to refine our app, Atlas:

  • Problem and Vision Statements:
    Problem Statement (From the stakeholder perspective) The current state of the traveling apps has focused primarily to help users find affordable and efficient traveling options. Services such as: Airbnb, Kayak and TripAdvisor fail to directly address a user’s time frame and overall travel budget (transportation, place to stay, food etc.) Our product/service will address this gap by allowing the user to search based on their budget and time available.

  • Vision Statement:
    From the user’s perspective, The new design of Atlas will help users achieve planning a trip by allowing them to plan based off their time and budget, with greater efficiency, and without problems of search limitations that they currently experience. This will dramatically improve Atlas’ customer satisfaction ratings which will lead user’s to our product. (BP)

  • Persona Expectations
    By integrating Persona needs with Problem and Vision Statements, we defined what Atlas should ideally offer to both Primary and Secondary Personas in terms of data and functional needs. (PP - primary , BP - both)

**ADD PICS** Frameworks

In the Frameworks Phase, our team focused on constructing a low-fidelity wireframe of the app using Figjam. We started with a Key Path Scenario, marked in red, to outline the most common and crucial journey of the Primary Persona through the app. This scenario was pivotal in highlighting the app's essential features and functionalities. Additionally, we integrated multiple Validation Scenarios, shown in purple, into the wireframe. These scenarios addressed the less frequently used features of the app, contributing to a richer user experience. This phase was crucial for establishing the app's foundational structure, enabling us to identify and rectify potential issues early in the design process. The low-fidelity wireframe served as a visual guide to understand the app's flow and page organization, setting a clear path for further refinement and development in subsequent phases.

Refinement

In the Refinement Phase of our project, the team undertook two key usability tests to assess the prototype's effectiveness and user-friendliness. These tests revealed several areas for improvement. Notably, color adjustments were necessary to enhance user experience, and the addition of extra screens was identified as a requirement to emulate a more authentic booking process. The team's focus in this phase was on refining these elements to ensure that the final product not only met the project's requirements but also provided an optimal and seamless user experience.

Conclusion

In my first experience with Goal Directed Design (GDD), I learned valuable lessons about the intricacies and challenges of the design process. Recognizing the importance of each phase in GDD, I found that skipping any step could significantly impact the final outcome. Central to this approach was the user research interviews, which form the foundation of app development.

Key Learnings:

  1. Leadership Challenges: Leading a team for the first time in a GDD project presented difficulties. Despite having a supportive professor, I often felt lost and realized the importance of asking even the smallest questions throughout the process.
  2. Research Interview Missteps: Under time pressure, I expedited the interview process without a specific demographic focus, leading to a less targeted approach in app development. In hindsight, focusing on college students as our primary demographic would have aligned better with Atlas's initial concept.
  3. Design Conflicts: Some aspects of the Atlas app, particularly certain pages, seem visually incomplete. This issue might have been mitigated with more time for refinement and development.

These experiences have been instrumental in shaping my understanding of the GDD process and the critical role of thorough, focused research and thoughtful leadership in successful app design.
